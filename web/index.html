SCOTUS 2.0

In total we found 677 URLs in the opinions. The first URL appeared in 1996 and the latest appeared on July 3, 2014 (where our dataset ended).

Of the 677 URLs we found, 315, or 47% are no longer pointing to the material they cite (link/reference rot).

Figure 1, Citations to the Web per year

We found that web archivsts have been busy. Of the 677 URLs, 546, or 81% have been archived by a web archiving service. 230 of our 315 rotten URLs, or 73%, have an archive available on the Web.

Figure 2, Percentage of Citations to the Web per year

It's no suprise that the older opinions have more rotten web citations.

Figure 3, Distribution of archives per year

For each link we looked for an archive using the Memento Timetravel service. As expected, the Internet Archive (and its Archive-It service) is deep. archive.today seems to have coverage going a ways back, even though the service hasn't been around all that long. How'd that happen? And the National Archives seem to have some coverage, but not all that much. How do they choose what to archive?

Two ways to count

The recent Hiberlink study focused on articles instead of links, finding that 70% of the 1.8 million science, technology, and math articles with web links published between 1997 and 2012 suffer from link or reference rot. If use this same zoom level, we find that 61% of opinions suffer from link or reference rot.

SCOTUS 1.0 links still decaying

The original study found that 50% of citations to the Web were no longer available. Using approximately the same 555 links used in the original study, we found that 54% of the citations to the Web are no longer available.

Since the original study,

14.5% (16) of URLs from decisions issued after 2012 are rotten.

81.8% (90) of URLs from decisions issued after 2012 have been archived.

62.5% (10) of rotten URLs from decisions issued after 2012 have been archived.
